- **Chapter 12: It's All about Complex, Entangled Networks** — Pessoa 2022, *The Entangled Brain*, pp 215-229. Parent ref: [[wiki/refs/pessoa-2022-entangled-brain]]. Final synthesis.

- ## TL;DR
  - The chapter that *consolidates the argument* and tells the reader what's needed next. Five major moves: (1) reorganisation-of-conserved-circuits as the right view of brain evolution; (2) a critique of constrained experimental paradigms ("fitting behavior inside a 40×40×40 centimeter box"); (3) parsimony is *not as easy as it sounds* — what counts as "simple" is community-dependent; (4) **causation in complex systems requires fundamentally different framing** (billiard balls vs trajectories, mutual causality, three-body problem); (5) **process ontology** — the world is made of processes, not things.
  - Pessoa ends with a call for a *phase transition* in neuroscience: from reductionist localisation to dynamic-networked-process thinking. Bigger and shinier tools won't save the field; conceptual development is required.
  - **Most quotable claim**: "Ultimately, to explain the cognitive-emotional brain, we need to dissolve boundaries within the brain — perception, cognition, action, emotion, motivation — as well as outside the brain, as we bring down the walls between biology, psychology, ecology, mathematics, computer science, philosophy, and so on."

- ## What Does Brain Evolution Mean?

  - **Reorganisation-of-conserved-circuits** as Pessoa's preferred frame for brain evolution.
  - Counter to "uniquely human" obsession: biologists have long sought one-of-a-kind features (specific brain regions, cell types, layered cortex) that distinguish humans. Most of these claims survive scrutiny poorly. Even the six-layered "neocortex" has antecedents in turtle dorsal pallium.
  - **The right move**: instead of asking "what's unique to humans?", ask "*how have conserved circuits been reorganised across taxa to support the species' particular survival challenges*?"
  - Direct implication for "amygdala does fear in rats and humans": the amygdala is *homologous* across rodents and primates but rodents and primates diverged 70+ million years ago — expecting identical function is implausible. Pessoa quotes Dobzhansky's "nothing makes sense in biology except in light of evolution" but warns that practising neuroscientists are often *cavalier* with evolutionary reasoning.

- ## Behaviour Constrained by 40×40×40 cm Boxes

  - The dominant experimental paradigms in neuroscience (classical conditioning chambers, monkey chairs, fMRI tubes) place severe limits on what behaviour can be studied.
  - Pessoa quotes Paré & Quirk: *"When a rat is presented with only one threatening stimulus in a testing box that allows for a single reflexive behavioral response, one is bound to find exactly what the experimental situation allows."*
  - **Tunnel-vision diagnosis**: the very success of the constrained-paradigm approach has led to shortsightedness. The amygdala-as-fear-centre story is partly an *artefact* of using a paradigm that allows only fear-like responses.
  - **Naturalistic paradigms** are starting to undermine standard stories: rats foraging near a robot predator show amygdala neurons that *reduce* firing during approach (not increase), inconsistent with simple threat coding. Mice exploring open fields show amygdala neurons tracking moment-to-moment exploration state, not "global anxiety" as previously assumed.
  - Calcium-imaging studies recording 24,000 neurons across 34 brain regions during a thirst task: a "go" odour triggers a *brain-wide cascade* within 300 ms — but only when the animal is thirsty. **State-dependent global propagation, not localised processing.**
  - Stringer et al. study of >10,000 V1 neurons during facial-movement filming: motor information is encoded *throughout* the cortex including primary visual cortex — directly contradicting the "sensory and motor info merge only in higher cortical areas" textbook view.

- ## Parsimony Is Easier Said Than Done

  - **Occam's razor** — *pluralitas non est ponenda sine necessitate* — sounds like a clean methodological principle, but the *what counts as parsimonious* depends on the community of scientists.
  - **Dark matter vs Modified Gravity (MOND)**: Pessoa's example. Vera Rubin (1978) found stars in galactic outskirts rotate too fast for observed mass + standard gravity. Two solutions:
    - {Standard Gravity G + Dark Matter m}: posit ~85% of universe is unobservable particles. *Massive* ontological commitment.
    - {Modified Gravity G + new constant a₀} (Milgrom 1983): small change to gravity equation. *Smaller* ontological commitment.
    - Physicists overwhelmingly chose option 1. Why? Pessoa: "What is considered frugal in theoretical terms depends very much on the intellectual mindset of a community of scientists."
  - **Application to neuroscience**: the choice between "complex network framework" and "localised processing" is *not* objectively settled by parsimony. Both communities can call the other wasteful. The choice is conceptual + cultural, made through paper acceptance, funding, conference selection.
  - Quote: "The position to embrace or shun complex systems is a collective viewpoint. To some, network-based explanations are too unwieldy and lacking in parsimony. In diametrical contrast, explanations heavily focused on localized circuits can be deemed as oversimplistic reductionism, or purely naive."

- ## Causation in Complex Systems

  - ### The "billiard ball model" of causation
    - Newtonian: ball A hits ball B, B moves. Translated to neuroscience: signal external to region 1 excites neurons → those neurons excite/inhibit region 2 via anatomical pathway → region 2 produces output.
    - **Problem**: this reasoning fails for the brain because of two structural features:
      - **Bidirectional connections**: most cortical-cortical and cortical-subcortical pathways are bidirectional. A causes B *and* B causes A. The basic concept of unidirectional causation breaks down.
      - **Convergence of projections**: multiple regions concurrently influence a single receiving node. *Unitary* causal influence is not assignable.

  - ### Mutual causality
    - Two boards leaning against each other in a Λ shape — each holds the other up. Neither is the "cause" of the other's position. They co-determine.
    - Pessoa cites philosophers' "mutual causality" as the relevant framework but doesn't fully develop it.

  - ### Three-body problem and the limits of analytic solution
    - **Two-body problem** (Bernoulli 1734): solved analytically.
    - **Three-body problem**: no general analytic solution exists. *The maths gets dramatically harder when adding just one more interacting object.*
    - **Implication for the brain**: with hundreds of regions interacting, analytic causal decomposition is hopeless. *Numerical simulation* (which is what computational models do) is the right tool, not closed-form causal narratives.

- ## The World Is Made of Processes, Not Things

  - **Heraclitus, Whitehead, organicists**: a 2500-year-old tradition that takes flow / process / activity as fundamental, with "things" being *stable processes*.
  - Quote (Heraclitus paraphrase): "Reality is not a constellation of things at all, but one of processes ... the river is not an object, but a continuing flow ... the sun is not a thing, but an enduring fire."
  - **Conrad Waddington (geneticist, organicist)**: "biology does not study things; it studies processes occurring at various timescales."
  - **Implication for neuroscience**:
    - The compulsion to define areas and subareas *recedes*, replaced by deciphering *how processes involving multiple parts of the brain unfold temporally*.
    - The brain region is no longer the object of analysis; the *trajectory* in high-dimensional state space is.

- ## Transient Brain Dynamics — Trajectories as the Unit

  - For n brain regions, define x_i(t) = activity of region i at time t. **The vector x(t) ∈ ℝⁿ is the state of the brain.**
  - Two tasks (A, B) → two trajectories in this state space (figure 12.2). Each trajectory is a *signature* of the task.
  - **Generalisation**: with n recorded regions, we have an (n+1)-dimensional spatiotemporal signature (n spatial + 1 temporal).
  - **Why this matters**:
    - *The trajectory is the object of interest*, not the region.
    - The framing is *spatially distributed* (no localisation needed) and *dynamic* (no static snapshot).
    - It moves us away from billiard-ball causation: no single factor "causes" the trajectory; the trajectory emerges from the system's evolution.
    - **Pessoa's caveat**: trajectories are descriptive, not yet explanatory. We still need *computational models* of brain function to explain why one trajectory differs from another. But the trajectory framing is *prerequisite* — we have to be able to talk about the right object before we can explain it.

- ## Final Thoughts — The Phase Transition

  - "Modern neuroscience has done so in a preponderantly reductionistic fashion for over a century and a half. ... I would venture that progress has been stymied by such approach and that the time is ripe for the field to *phase-transition* into a period when a truly dynamic and networked view of the brain takes hold."
  - **Four fronts to advance on**: dynamics, decentralised computation, emergence, competition.
  - **Critical complement**: a theoretical-computational foundation. *"Bigger and shinier tools and techniques alone won't yield the necessary progress; we run the risk of being able to measure every cell (or subcellular component even) in the brain in a theoretical vacuum."*
  - **Boundaries to dissolve**:
    - *Within the brain*: perception, cognition, action, emotion, motivation as distinct categories. They aren't.
    - *Outside the brain*: biology / psychology / ecology / mathematics / computer science / philosophy as separate fields. Brain understanding requires breaking these walls down.
